**Counter-Strike 2** (CS2) is Valve's tactical first-person shooter and the fifth mainline entry in a franchise that has run continuously since 1999. It is free to play, Windows- and Linux-only, and consistently the most-played game on Steam.[^quantumrun2026] It is also the backbone of one of the oldest and most durable esports scenes in existence: a professional circuit with its own ranking systems, its own economy, its own press, and its own long-running scandals.

This entry is written for someone who knows what Counter-Strike *is* but has never followed it; no history with the pro scene, no familiarity with the jargon, no idea which teams matter or why anyone cares. It covers the game's lineage, how a competitive season actually works, the two teams worth anchoring on, the people worth watching, the controversies that shape the culture, and where to go next. Terminology is defined as it appears.

## From 1.6 to Source 2

Counter-Strike began in 1999 as a mod for *Half-Life*, built by two hobbyists and eventually bought outright by Valve. The formula it landed on (two teams of five, one planting a bomb and one defusing it, no respawns, an economy that carries over between rounds) has barely changed in twenty-five years. Everything since has been iteration on that core.

The lineage runs *Counter-Strike 1.6*, *Condition Zero*, *Counter-Strike: Source*, then **Counter-Strike: Global Offensive** (CS:GO), released in 2012 on the Source engine. CS:GO is the version most people have heard of. It launched as a $15 game, went free-to-play in December 2018, and became a cultural phenomenon largely on the back of something orthogonal to gameplay: the Arms Deal update of August 2013, which introduced **skins**, purely cosmetic weapon finishes obtained by opening paid **cases**. Skins were tradable on the Steam marketplace, which made them liquid, which made them an asset class, which made them a gambling substrate. That decision echoes through everything in this entry.

On 27 September 2023, Valve replaced CS:GO with Counter-Strike 2. This was not a sequel launch in the ordinary sense. The new game shipped as a free in-place upgrade to the same Steam entry, CS:GO was delisted, and player inventories carried over. The game moved to the **Source 2** engine and brought two headline changes. **Sub-tick networking** means the server registers the exact moment an action occurred rather than snapping it to the nearest server tick, so shots and movement resolve on their true timing. **Volumetric smoke** replaced CS:GO's flat smoke sprites with a dynamic volume that reacts to the world; bullets carve temporary holes through it, grenades displace it, and it fills space realistically rather than clipping through geometry.

The transition was rough. Early CS2 shipped with fewer features than the game it replaced, and cheating, hit registration, and matchmaking complaints dominated the discourse for over a year. The game has since stabilised. As of 2026 it typically holds 700,000 to 900,000 average concurrent players with daily peaks well above a million, and it recorded an all-time peak of 1,862,531 concurrent players on 12 April 2025.[^steamdb2026] Estimated 2025 revenue, almost entirely case keys and Steam Market transaction fees, was around $1.16 billion.[^quantumrun2026]

Valve still ships regular updates. The 2026 season has brought a bomb-damage rework in which C4 explosion damage is precomputed per map and the shockwave expands outward from the centre rather than applying instantly, an ongoing map-pool rotation, and a recoil-camera adjustment intended to bring the feel closer to CS:GO while preserving CS2's bullet trajectories.[^valve2026]

## How a match works, and what the words mean

A competitive match is 5v5. One team plays **Terrorists** (T), whose objective is to plant a bomb, the **C4**, on one of two bombsites and defend it until detonation. The other plays **Counter-Terrorists** (CT), who must prevent the plant or defuse the bomb after it is planted. Eliminating the entire opposing team also wins the round. Players do not respawn mid-round; once you are dead, you spectate. Teams swap sides at halftime, so every team must be competent on both.

The **economy** is the mechanic that makes Counter-Strike a strategy game rather than an aim contest. Money is awarded for winning rounds, losing rounds (a scaling *loss bonus*), planting the bomb, and getting kills. Weapons and equipment are bought fresh at the start of each round and lost on death. This creates round types with their own names: a **full buy** (everyone can afford rifles and utility), an **eco** (deliberately spending almost nothing to bank money for a later round), and a **force buy** (spending everything now, accepting a weaker loadout, because the round matters).

**Utility** means grenades: smoke, flashbang (a *flash*), high-explosive, incendiary or Molotov, and decoy. Elite Counter-Strike is largely a contest of utility usage; a smoke thrown to the exact pixel, at the exact second, from the exact spot, to cut a defender's sightline. Lineups for these throws are memorised and drilled.

Roles within a team are informal but consistent:

- **IGL** (in-game leader). The shot-caller. Draws up strategy, calls mid-round adjustments. Often the weakest fragger on the team because the mental load is enormous.
- **AWPer**. The sniper. The **AWP** is a one-shot-kill rifle that costs a fortune; a team's AWPer is usually its highest-paid star.
- **Entry fragger**. Opens sites, takes the first duel, dies a lot on purpose.
- **Lurker**. Plays away from the team, catching rotations from behind.
- **Support**. Throws the utility that makes everyone else's job possible. Statistically invisible, tactically essential.

A **clutch** is winning a round alone against multiple opponents (a *1v3*, *1v5*). An **ace** is killing all five opponents in a round. **Spray control** is compensating for a weapon's recoil pattern, which in Counter-Strike is fixed and learnable rather than random.

On the statistics side, **HLTV Rating** is the community-standard player metric, combining kills, deaths, damage, and **KAST**, the percentage of rounds in which a player got a kill, an assist, survived, or was traded. **ADR** is average damage per round. A rating around 1.00 is average for a professional; sustained figures above 1.20 mark a superstar.

Competitively, matches are played as a **Bo1** (single map), **Bo3**, or **Bo5**. Map selection happens through a **veto**, an alternating ban-and-pick process from the active map pool. **Big Event** is the informal designation for a tournament significant enough to count toward year-end rankings.

## The competitive structure

The professional scene has two overlapping ranking systems, and understanding the difference explains almost everything about how the circuit behaves.

**Valve Regional Standings** (VRS) is Valve's official ranking. It is Elo-style, weights recent results heavily over roughly a six-month window, and factors in opponent strength and prize money earned. Teams are sorted into three regions (Europe, the Americas, and Asia) that feed a global leaderboard.[^egamersworld2026] The crucial fact is that since 2025, Valve has required tournament organisers to allocate invitations by VRS rather than by partnership or franchise agreement. Tier-one events must invite from the top of the leaderboard; tier-two events start further down. This was a deliberate structural intervention. It ended an era in which the biggest tournaments invited their business partners, and it made rank a literal gate on opportunity. Slip a few places and you lose direct invites, which costs you the chance to earn the points that would restore your rank.

**HLTV World Ranking** is the community benchmark, published every Monday by the site [HLTV.org](https://www.hltv.org/ranking/teams) since 2015. It weights achievements over the past year with heavy monthly decay, recent form over two months, and performance at recent LAN events, and it requires a team to retain a three-player core to keep its points.[^liquipedia2026] It carries no official weight. It carries enormous cultural weight. VRS decides who gets in; HLTV decides who is respected.

The pinnacle of the circuit is the **Major**, a Valve-sponsored world championship, the only S-tier event class, currently held twice a year with a $1,250,000 prize pool and 32 teams.[^esportsgg2026] Everything else is third-party: ESL (IEM Katowice, IEM Cologne, ESL Pro League), BLAST, PGL, and others. Majors are also the scene's financial engine, because they are the only events at which Valve sells in-game tournament **stickers** bearing team and player signatures, with a share of the revenue flowing back to the organisers, teams, and players. A player sticker on a Major-winning roster is both a payday and a form of permanence.

Notably, the largest prize pool of the 2026 season does not belong to a Major at all. The Esports World Cup, held in Saudi Arabia in August, carries $2,000,000.[^esportsgg2026] Saudi capital now outspends Valve on Counter-Strike, which is not a neutral fact and is addressed below.

### A season, start to finish

A season is a half-year cycle that terminates in a Major. In practice it runs like this:

1. The cycle opens immediately after the previous Major ends.
2. Teams compete across the Valve-sanctioned third-party circuit: IEM, BLAST, PGL, ESL Pro League, and smaller events. Every result feeds VRS.
3. VRS accumulates. Placement, opponent strength, and prize money all contribute; recent results dominate.
4. Rank gates access. High rank means direct invites to tier-one events; low rank means grinding qualifiers for the chance to earn points at all.
5. Valve freezes the standings on a set date. The top teams per region receive Major invitations, 32 in total. There are no longer separate regional qualifier tournaments; the ranking alone decides.
6. Rosters lock roughly six weeks before the Major. No further substitutions.
7. The Major itself runs three Swiss-system stages. In Swiss, teams are paired against opponents with the same record; three wins advance, three losses eliminate. Early matches are Bo1, decisive matches Bo3.
8. The top eight from the final stage enter a single-elimination Bo3 playoff bracket. The grand final is Bo3 or Bo5.
9. A champion is crowned, champion stickers go on sale, and points reset for the next window.

The [IEM Cologne Major 2026](https://www.hltv.org/events/8301/iem-cologne-major-2026), the fifth CS2 Major, ran from 2 to 21 June in Cologne, Germany, with playoffs at the Lanxess Arena. Team Falcons beat FURIA 3-0 in the grand final for their first Major title, with Ilya "m0NESY" Osipov taking MVP. It was the first Major at which every match in Stage 3 was played as a Bo3.[^wikipedia2026a] The next Major is PGL Major Singapore, running 12 November to 13 December 2026.[^esportsgg2026]

## Two teams to anchor on

The scene has perhaps eight teams that matter at any given moment. Two are enough to start with.

### Team Vitality

[Team Vitality](https://vitality.gg/) is the defining team of the CS2 era and the reason most conversations about "greatness" in modern Counter-Strike start and end in France. The roster consists of [Dan "apEX" Madesclaire](https://www.hltv.org/player/7322/apex) (IGL), [Mathieu "ZywOo" Herbaut](https://www.hltv.org/player/11893/zywoo) (AWP), [Robin "ropz" Kool](https://www.hltv.org/player/11816/ropz), [Shahar "flameZ" Shushan](https://www.hltv.org/player/16693/flamez), and [William "mezii" Merriman](https://www.hltv.org/player/18462/mezii). They were the only top team in 2025 to field the same five players all year.

The 2025 season is the case for them. Across sixteen Big Events they finished first nine times, twice at Majors, and only once finished worse than fourth. They ran a thirty-match win streak. They won BLAST.tv Austin in June and StarLadder Budapest in December, becoming the first team since the Astralis dynasty of 2019 to take back-to-back Majors, and the first since 2019 to win two Majors in a single calendar year.[^hltv2026a] ZywOo was named HLTV Player of the Year, his fourth, a record no one else has approached.

The engine is not just ZywOo. apEX is regarded as one of the best in-game leaders of the era, the team runs a multi-layer coaching structure under [Rémy "XTQZZZ" Quoniam](https://www.hltv.org/player/9086/xtqzzz), and roster stability has compounded into something opponents describe as suffocating. They entered 2026 still at the top of both ranking systems, though Falcons broke an eighteen-match win streak in April and eliminated them en route to the Cologne Major title, which has finally introduced a question mark.

### Natus Vincere

[Natus Vincere](https://navi.gg/) (NAVI) is the scene's most storied brand, a Ukrainian organisation that has been near the top of Counter-Strike for over a decade, and the team that carried [Oleksandr "s1mple" Kostyliev](https://www.hltv.org/player/7998/s1mple), still widely considered one of the greatest players in the game's history. NAVI were HLTV's Team of the Year in 2021 and again in 2024, winning the PGL Major Copenhagen in 2024.[^hltv2026a]

They are currently in transition. The roster is [Aleksi "Aleksib" Virolainen](https://www.hltv.org/player/9816/aleksib) (IGL), [Mihai "iM" Ivan](https://www.hltv.org/player/14759/im), [Valerii "b1t" Vakhovskyi](https://www.hltv.org/player/18987/b1t), [Ihor "w0nderful" Zhdanov](https://www.hltv.org/player/20127/w0nderful), and the teenage prospect [Drin "makazze" Shaqiri](https://www.hltv.org/player/22673/makazze), promoted from the academy mid-2025.[^hltv2026b] They remain a top-five team by VRS but have not matched their 2024 peak, and the transfer rumour mill has circled them constantly.

NAVI is the useful counterweight to Vitality precisely because they are *not* dominant. They are a legacy organisation navigating a rebuild in public, under a fanbase whose patience is finite. Watching them is watching how a great team ages.

## Players worth knowing

[ZywOo](https://www.hltv.org/player/11893/zywoo) (Vitality) is the cleanest expression of elite Counter-Strike: a French AWPer with four Player of the Year titles. [donk](https://www.hltv.org/player/21167/donk) (Spirit) is the opposite; a Russian entry fragger, still a teenager, who produces the highest raw statistical output in the game and can singlehandedly flip maps. [m0NESY](https://www.hltv.org/player/19230/m0nesy) (Falcons) is the third pillar, a Russian AWPer who took MVP at the Cologne Major.[^wikipedia2026a] [NiKo](https://www.hltv.org/player/3741/niko) (Falcons) is the veteran superstar rifler, arguably the best pure mechanical player of the last decade, who spent years chasing a Major and only won one in 2026.

## Controversies

Counter-Strike's scandals are not incidental to the scene. They *are* the scene, in the sense that its economy, its media, and its player incentives are all shaped by them.

### The gambling problem

This is the root controversy and everything else is downstream. Skins are cosmetic, but because they are tradable and liquid, they function as currency. An entire offshore industry of skin-betting and case-opening sites grew up around CS:GO and persists in CS2. In 2016, skin-gambling drew regulatory scrutiny and a wave of cease-and-desist letters from Valve after it emerged that prominent streamers were secretly promoting sites they owned. It did not go away.

Twitch's community guidelines explicitly prohibit promotion or sponsorship of skin gambling, yet permit streamers to open cases live, a distinction widely regarded as a loophole, since a case is functionally a loot box with a market-priced payout.[^gamblingnews2025] Meanwhile Majors and tier-one events carry bookmaker sponsorships, players hold affiliate codes for case sites, and a substantial fraction of the CS2 news ecosystem, including the sites that publish rankings, roster news, and community coverage, is owned or funded by skin marketplaces and gambling operators. The most-cited criticism of the community is that it condemns casino streams while celebrating six-figure case openings, which are the same thing wearing a different hat.[^skinclub2025]

### Match-fixing and cheating

The iBUYPOWER match-fixing scandal of 2015 resulted in permanent competitive bans for an entire American roster and remains the scene's original sin. The 2020 "coach bug", a spectator-camera exploit that let coaches see parts of the map they should not, implicated dozens of coaches across the professional circuit when it was uncovered, producing one of the largest disciplinary actions in esports history. Ordinary cheating in matchmaking remains unsolved. VAC, the Overwatch community-review system, Prime Status, and Trust Factor have all reduced it without eliminating it.

### Team controversies

**Vitality** are, unusually, close to clean. No scandal attaches to the roster or organisation. The complaints against them are about dominance itself: fans tired of one-sided finals, a viral Reddit thread asking the community to design an unlimited-budget roster capable of beating them (the most common response being that no lineup can, because three of the world's five best players are already on Vitality), and grumbling about apEX and mezii publicly claiming greatest-of-all-time status.[^skinclub2026] This is the healthiest kind of controversy a team can have.

**Natus Vincere** carry two. In mid-2022 the organisation removed its in-game leader Kirill "Boombl4" Mikhailov, citing what it described as high reputational risks for the club; an abrupt, public, never-fully-explained separation that occurred immediately after the Antwerp Major.[^hltv2022] The second is structural and ongoing. NAVI is a Ukrainian organisation fielding a multinational, largely Russian-speaking roster during a war, and no amount of competitive success makes that tension disappear.

Further afield, two cases are worth knowing because they define the outer bounds of the scene's tolerance. [Team Falcons](https://www.hltv.org/team/11283/falcons), the reigning Major champions, are Saudi-owned. Their aggressive assembly of superteams has been widely characterised as sportswashing, and [Russel "Twistzz" Van Dulken](https://www.hltv.org/player/10394/twistzz) publicly declined to join, saying that if he wanted the money and did not care about his career he would sign, but he has morals.[^wikipedia2026b] And in 2022, [G2 Esports](https://www.hltv.org/team/5995/g2) founder and CEO Carlos "ocelote" Rodríguez posted a video of himself partying with Andrew Tate, was suspended, and resigned within a week amid sustained backlash from fans, staff, and Riot Games.[^pcgamer2022]

## Content creators

The creator ecosystem splits cleanly into educators, entertainers, and pros, and the fault line between them is, again, gambling.

[**WarOwl**](https://www.youtube.com/@TheWarOwl) is the educator and the safest entry point in the entire scene. He has been making Counter-Strike tutorials for over a decade (maps, weapons, fundamentals, economy) pitched squarely at players who have never touched the game, and he posts breakdowns whenever a significant update lands. He is approaching 1.5 million YouTube subscribers.[^jaxon2025] There is no controversy attached to him, which in this scene is itself notable.

[**ohnePixel**](https://www.twitch.tv/ohnepixel) (Mark Zimmermann) is the most influential CS personality alive and the most contested. A German streamer who began as a skin trader in 2019, he is now the definitive voice on the CS economy (case openings, rare-item history, market analysis) and a major tournament co-streamer.[^win2026] He is also, in the view of a substantial slice of the community, personally responsible for turning Counter-Strike into a slot machine; Steam discussion threads accusing him of ruining the game are a recurring genre. In his defence, he has stated publicly that he turned down a skin-gambling sponsorship worth roughly a million dollars a month, on the explicit grounds that such money comes from people's addictions.[^dexerto2023] He has also drawn criticism for how he handled an on-stream interview in which the former professional Jake "Stewie2K" Yip denied scamming accusations from the trading community; viewers accused him of running interference.[^escorenews2025]

[**Anomaly**](https://www.youtube.com/@Anomaly) is the long-running entertainment creator; funny gameplay, skin content, case openings at enormous scale. He sits at the centre of the same critique as ohnePixel, and community accusations that the large case-opening streamers exploit in-game reward systems and distort skin prices are a persistent Steam-forum genre, though Valve has taken no action on them.[^steamforum2024] The honest framing is that case-opening content, however entertaining, is advertising for a gambling-adjacent product, and Anomaly's audience skews young.

[**NiKo**](https://www.twitch.tv/niko) is included here as the pro-who-streams: rare broadcasts, but a masterclass in aim and positioning when they happen. His controversy is not personal conduct but affiliation. He joined Team Falcons in 2025 and has been part of the Saudi superteam project ever since, the same project Twistzz publicly refused on principle. For years the accompanying story was that Falcons could buy any player and still not win; they reached seven grand finals between January 2025 and mid-2026 and won one of them, until Cologne.[^wikipedia2026b]

## Where to follow the scene

Two sources cover almost everything, and both are free of the skin-economy conflict of interest that taints most CS media.

[**HLTV.org**](https://www.hltv.org/) is the scene's centre of gravity: news, match schedules, live results, the [World Ranking](https://www.hltv.org/ranking/teams), a mirror of the [Valve ranking](https://www.hltv.org/valve-ranking/teams), player statistics, and the [forums](https://www.hltv.org/forums/counterstrike), which are simultaneously the most knowledgeable and the most unhinged community on the internet. If a roster move happens, HLTV has it first. If a match is being played, the HLTV thread is where it is being argued about.

[**Liquipedia**](https://liquipedia.net/counterstrike/Main_Page) is the structured archive: tournament brackets, complete roster histories, [year-end rankings](https://liquipedia.net/counterstrike/Portal:Rankings) going back over a decade, transaction logs, prize-pool records. Where HLTV is the newspaper, Liquipedia is the library. It is community-maintained, thoroughly sourced, and the correct first stop for any factual question about the game's competitive history.

Both publish RSS feeds. Anyone running a self-hosted reader can pull HLTV's news feed and Liquipedia's changes alongside [SteamDB's patch-note feed](https://steamdb.info/app/730/patchnotes/) and skip the advertising-saturated aggregators entirely.
