**Encryption** is the mathematical process of transforming readable data into an unintelligible form that only an authorized party — one who possesses the correct key — can reverse. It is the foundational mechanism behind secure web browsing, private messaging, encrypted storage, and authenticated communications. Every time a browser loads an HTTPS page, a VPN tunnels traffic, or a messaging app sends a private message, encryption is operating beneath the surface, converting information into ciphertext for any would-be interceptor while restoring it faithfully for the intended recipient.[^proton2024]

Understanding encryption requires distinguishing it from related concepts. *Encoding* (such as Base64) transforms data into a different representation for compatibility and is freely reversible without any secret — it provides no security. Encryption is deliberately different: without the key, decryption is computationally infeasible, not merely inconvenient. This intentional asymmetry between the ease of encryption and the difficulty of unauthorized decryption is the source of its security value.

## Core components

Four components are present in every encryption scheme. **Plaintext** is the original, human-readable data: a password, a document, a payment record. **Ciphertext** is the output after encryption — an apparently random sequence of bytes that reveals nothing about its contents. The **algorithm** is the fixed set of mathematical operations that transforms plaintext into ciphertext; by convention, algorithms are published and subject to public cryptanalysis, not kept secret. The **key** is a string of bits that serves as the variable input to the algorithm, making the output unique to that key even when the algorithm is universally known.[^ibm2024]

Key length determines brute-force resistance and scales exponentially. A 128-bit key has on the order of \(3.4 \times 10^{38}\) possible values; exhausting that space at billions of attempts per second would require longer than the current age of the universe. Encryption's security model rests entirely on key secrecy, not algorithmic secrecy — a principle known as Kerckhoffs's principle, which holds that a system should remain secure even if everything about it except the key is public knowledge.

## Symmetric encryption

**Symmetric encryption** uses a single shared key for both encryption and decryption. Its primary advantage is speed: the mathematical operations involved are computationally lightweight, making symmetric ciphers suitable for encrypting large volumes of data such as disk contents, VPN traffic, and database records.[^ibm_sym2024]

The dominant algorithm is the **Advanced Encryption Standard (AES)**, standardized by NIST in FIPS 197 following a public competition intended to replace the aging Data Encryption Standard, whose 56-bit key had become trivially brute-forceable.[^nist2001] AES is a **block cipher**: it processes data in fixed 128-bit blocks. It supports three key lengths — 128, 192, and 256 bits — corresponding to 10, 12, and 14 transformation rounds respectively. Each round applies four operations to a 4×4 matrix of bytes: *SubBytes* replaces each byte with its multiplicative inverse in a finite field, introducing non-linearity; *ShiftRows* cyclically offsets each row, spreading bytes laterally; *MixColumns* multiplies each column as a polynomial, diffusing data so that every output bit depends on many input bits; and *AddRoundKey* XORs the result with material derived from the key schedule. This **substitution-permutation network** structure ensures that a single-bit change in either the plaintext or the key cascades unpredictably throughout the entire output — a property called the avalanche effect. AES-256 is approved by the U.S. government for the protection of Top Secret classified information.[^fips197]

Other symmetric algorithms remain relevant in specific contexts. *ChaCha20*, a stream cipher designed by Daniel Bernstein, encrypts data bit by bit rather than in blocks and is preferred on devices that lack hardware AES acceleration. *Triple DES (3DES)*, which applied the original DES cipher three times in sequence, was formally deprecated by NIST for all new software applications in 2023 due to insufficient security margins.

The structural weakness of symmetric encryption is the **key distribution problem**: both parties must possess the same secret key before communication begins, and transmitting that key over an insecure channel without prior arrangement appears circular. Asymmetric cryptography was invented specifically to resolve this.

## Asymmetric encryption

**Asymmetric encryption**, also called public-key cryptography, uses a mathematically linked pair of keys — a **public key** that anyone may freely obtain and a **private key** that only the owner holds. Data encrypted with a party's public key can be decrypted only with the corresponding private key. Conversely, data signed with a private key can be verified using the public key, forming the basis of digital signatures. The key insight is that the relationship between the keys is computationally one-directional: deriving the private key from the public key is infeasible in practice, even though both are mathematically related.

**RSA** (Rivest–Shamir–Adleman) is the most widely known asymmetric algorithm. Key generation begins by selecting two large prime numbers and multiplying them to produce a modulus \(n\). A public exponent \(e\) is chosen (commonly 65,537), and the private exponent \(d\) is computed as the modular inverse of \(e\) with respect to Euler's totient of \(n\). Encryption is modular exponentiation: ciphertext \(c = m^e \bmod n\), where \(m\) is the message. Decryption recovers the message as \(m = c^d \bmod n\). Security rests on the **integer factorization problem**: given \(n\), recovering its prime factors is computationally infeasible for sufficiently large keys. NIST recommends a minimum of 2,048-bit RSA keys, with 3,072 bits providing 128-bit equivalent security.[^nist_rsa]

**Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC)** has become the preferred alternative to RSA in most new deployments because it achieves equivalent security with dramatically smaller keys. ECC operates over curves of the form \(y^2 = x^3 + ax + b\) defined over a finite field. A publicly known base point \(G\) serves as a starting reference; a private key \(k\) is a random integer and the corresponding public key \(K\) is the result of the elliptic curve point multiplication \(K = kG\). Security rests on the **Elliptic Curve Discrete Logarithm Problem**: given \(G\) and \(K\), recovering \(k\) is computationally infeasible. The practical consequence is that a 256-bit ECC key provides security equivalent to a 3,072-bit RSA key, making ECC particularly attractive for resource-constrained environments such as smartphones and embedded devices.[^techtarget_ecc] Common ECC-based schemes include ECDSA for digital signatures, ECDH for key exchange, and EdDSA (Ed25519) for high-performance signing.

Because asymmetric operations are orders of magnitude slower than symmetric ones, they are almost never used to encrypt bulk data. Modern systems instead follow a **hybrid model**: asymmetric cryptography secures the exchange of a symmetric session key, and that session key handles encryption of the actual content. This design underlies TLS, PGP, and virtually every other secure communication protocol.

## Key generation and exchange

The security of any encryption scheme depends on the quality of its keys. Keys must be generated by a **Cryptographically Secure Pseudo-Random Number Generator (CSPRNG)** — a generator whose output is computationally indistinguishable from true randomness. NIST SP 800-90A standardizes three CSPRNG constructions: *Hash_DRBG* (based on SHA-256), *HMAC_DRBG* (based on the HMAC construction), and *CTR_DRBG* (based on AES in counter mode). These generators are seeded with entropy harvested from physical sources such as thermal noise in circuits, interrupt timing jitter, and hardware random number generator instructions.[^csprng_wiki] A CSPRNG must satisfy the *next-bit test*: given the first \(k\) bits of output, no efficient algorithm may predict the \((k+1)\)th bit with probability greater than 50%.[^nakov2024]

Secure key agreement between strangers is achieved through **Diffie-Hellman key exchange**. Two parties publicly agree on a large prime \(p\) and a generator \(g\). Each independently generates a private random exponent, computes a public value by raising \(g\) to that exponent modulo \(p\), and transmits the result. Each party then raises the other's public value to their own private exponent modulo \(p\), arriving at the same shared secret. An eavesdropper who observes the exchange cannot efficiently compute the shared secret because doing so requires solving the **discrete logarithm problem**. The modern variant, **Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman (ECDH)**, replaces modular exponentiation with elliptic-curve point multiplication, achieving equivalent security with smaller parameters.[^sciencedirect_dh]

When new key pairs are generated for every session — a variant called **ECDHE** (ephemeral) — the protocol provides **perfect forward secrecy**: even if a long-term private key is later compromised, previously recorded sessions remain protected because the ephemeral keys used to derive their session keys were discarded immediately after use. The raw output of a Diffie-Hellman exchange is not used directly as an encryption key; instead it is processed through a **Key Derivation Function (KDF)** such as HKDF, which mixes the shared secret with a random salt and context-specific labels to produce keys with the precise length and distribution required by the encryption algorithm.

## Cryptographic hash functions

**Cryptographic hash functions** are related to encryption but serve a different purpose. They take an input of arbitrary size and produce a fixed-length output — called a **digest** or fingerprint — that deterministically represents that input. The transformation is one-way: recovering the input from the digest is computationally infeasible. Hash functions use no key and provide no confidentiality; their role is **integrity verification and authentication**.

**SHA-256**, part of the SHA-2 family designed by the NSA and standardized by NIST in FIPS 180-4, is the most widely deployed hash function.[^sha2_wiki] It processes input in 512-bit blocks through 64 rounds of operations involving bitwise logic, modular addition, and mixing with constants derived from the fractional parts of the cube roots of the first 64 primes. Every input, regardless of size, produces a 256-bit output. A well-designed hash function exhibits several essential properties: it is deterministic; it demonstrates the avalanche effect (a single-bit change in input produces a radically different digest); it is *pre-image resistant* (given a digest, finding any input that produces it is infeasible); and it is *collision resistant* (finding two different inputs with the same digest is infeasible).[^ssldragon2024]

Hash functions are used throughout cryptographic infrastructure: verifying file integrity by comparing a downloaded file's digest against a published reference value, storing passwords by retaining only the hash rather than the plaintext, constructing HMAC (Hash-based Message Authentication Code) for message authentication, and forming the digest that is signed in digital signature schemes.

## Digital signatures and certificates

A **digital signature** combines asymmetric cryptography and hash functions to simultaneously provide integrity, authenticity, and non-repudiation. To sign a message, the sender computes its SHA-256 digest and then encrypts that digest with their private key; the result is the signature, which is attached to the message. To verify, the recipient independently computes the digest of the received message and decrypts the signature using the sender's public key; if the two digests match, the message is confirmed both unaltered and originating from the claimed sender. Because only the private-key holder could have produced the signature, they cannot plausibly deny authorship.[^ibm2024]

Digital signatures raise a trust bootstrapping problem: how can a recipient know that the public key used for verification genuinely belongs to the claimed sender rather than an impersonator? The answer is **digital certificates** and the **Public Key Infrastructure (PKI)**. A digital certificate is an electronic document formatted according to the **X.509 standard** (defined in ITU-T X.509 and profiled by IETF RFC 5280) that binds a public key to an identity by including the subject's name, their public key, a validity period, and the digital signature of a **Certificate Authority (CA)** — a trusted third party that has verified the subject's identity.[^ssl_com2024]

Trust is organized as a hierarchy. Root CAs, whose self-signed certificates are pre-installed in operating systems and browsers, sit at the apex. Root CAs sign certificates for Intermediate CAs, which sign end-entity certificates for websites, individuals, or devices. When a browser connects to a server, it receives the server's certificate, traces the chain of signatures upward to a trusted root, and verifies every link. Certificates can be revoked before expiration via Certificate Revocation Lists (CRLs) or the Online Certificate Status Protocol (OCSP), so that compromised certificates are promptly distrusted.[^kusari2024]

## TLS and HTTPS

The **Transport Layer Security (TLS)** protocol, standardized in IETF RFC 8446 (TLS 1.3, 2018), is the most visible application of encryption on the internet and serves as a practical illustration of how symmetric ciphers, asymmetric cryptography, key exchange, hash functions, and certificates fit together into a working system.[^thesslstore2024]

A TLS 1.3 connection begins with a **Client Hello**, in which the browser sends the server its supported cipher suites (TLS 1.3 reduced the options to five secure choices, all requiring AES-GCM or ChaCha20-Poly1305), a random nonce, and pre-computed Diffie-Hellman key shares for the most likely key-exchange groups. By sending key material speculatively, TLS 1.3 reduces the handshake to a single round trip, compared to two in TLS 1.2.[^auth02024] The server responds with its own nonce, the selected cipher suite, its X.509 certificate, a digital signature demonstrating possession of the corresponding private key, and its ECDHE key share. At this point, both parties can independently derive the same shared secret via ECDHE and process it through HKDF to produce symmetric session keys. All subsequent traffic — including the server's Finished message — is immediately encrypted with those keys.[^commandline2024]

Once the handshake completes, all application data flows over **authenticated symmetric encryption** using an AEAD (Authenticated Encryption with Associated Data) construction. AEAD schemes such as AES-256-GCM simultaneously provide confidentiality, integrity, and authentication in a single pass; any tampering with the ciphertext causes decryption to fail explicitly rather than silently producing garbage output. TLS 1.3 mandates ephemeral key exchange for every session, making perfect forward secrecy a non-negotiable requirement rather than an option. For returning clients, **0-RTT resumption** allows application data to accompany the very first handshake message, though this mode accepts a limited replay-attack surface in exchange for reduced latency.

## End-to-end encryption

Standard TLS protects data in transit between a client and a server but leaves the server in possession of plaintext once the data arrives. **End-to-end encryption (E2EE)** extends the trust boundary to the endpoints themselves: data is encrypted on the sender's device and decrypted only on the recipient's device, ensuring that the relay server never holds the keys needed to read the content.[^cloudflare_e2ee]

The most widely deployed E2EE system is the **Signal Protocol**, developed by Open Whisper Systems in 2013 and subsequently adopted by WhatsApp, Google Messages, and Facebook Messenger.[^signal_wiki] Initial key agreement uses **X3DH (Extended Triple Diffie-Hellman)**: each user registers a long-term identity key, a medium-term signed pre-key, and a batch of single-use one-time pre-keys with the server. When a sender wants to initiate a conversation — even with an offline recipient — they download the recipient's public keys, generate an ephemeral key pair, and perform three or four Diffie-Hellman computations across different combinations of the available key pairs. The results are combined into a root key, providing both forward secrecy and cryptographic deniability.[^sentientrant2024]

Ongoing message encryption is handled by the **Double Ratchet Algorithm**, which runs two interlocking ratcheting mechanisms in parallel. A symmetric KDF chain ratchet advances after every message, producing a unique message key each time while the one-way nature of the KDF prevents backward derivation. An asymmetric Diffie-Hellman ratchet periodically injects fresh randomness by exchanging new ephemeral key pairs during the conversation, providing **post-compromise security** (also called self-healing): if an attacker temporarily gains access to a device's key state, the next DH ratchet step introduces entropy the attacker cannot control, locking them out of all future messages.[^stealthcloud2024]

The practical implication is significant: with TLS alone, a service provider can read, index, scan, or surrender user data under legal process. With properly implemented E2EE, the provider can produce only ciphertext it cannot decrypt. Metadata — who communicated with whom and when — may still be accessible, but message content remains protected. Proton extends this model to email, cloud storage, and calendar data using the OpenPGP standard. Signal has further hardened the protocol against future quantum adversaries with the Sparse Post-Quantum Ratchet (SPQR), which runs the classical Double Ratchet in parallel with a post-quantum key-encapsulation mechanism based on CRYSTALS-Kyber (ML-KEM), mixing their outputs for hybrid classical-quantum security.[^signal_pqc]

## Post-quantum considerations

Large-scale quantum computers, if realized, would undermine the mathematical hardness assumptions underlying RSA and ECC. Shor's algorithm can factor large integers and solve the discrete logarithm problem in polynomial time on a sufficiently powerful quantum computer, rendering both families of asymmetric algorithms insecure. Symmetric ciphers like AES are less affected: Grover's algorithm can search a keyspace in \(\sqrt{N}\) time rather than \(N\), effectively halving the security margin in bits — AES-256 is thus estimated to provide 128-bit post-quantum security and is expected to remain viable.[^nist2001]

NIST completed its post-quantum cryptography standardization process in 2024, publishing the first generation of post-quantum algorithms. *ML-KEM* (Module Lattice-based Key Encapsulation Mechanism, formerly CRYSTALS-Kyber) replaces asymmetric key exchange, and *ML-DSA* (formerly CRYSTALS-Dilithium) replaces digital signatures; both are based on the hardness of lattice problems, specifically the Module Learning With Errors (MLWE) problem, for which no efficient quantum algorithm is known. The transition guidance recommends hybrid deployments — running classical and post-quantum algorithms in parallel — during the migration period to guard against both classical and quantum adversaries simultaneously, a design already implemented in the Signal Protocol's SPQR extension.[^signal_pqc]
