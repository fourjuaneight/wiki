**Twisted-pair Ethernet cable** is the physical medium that carries digital data between devices on a local area network, and it forms the physical backbone of virtually every wired network in operation today. A standard cable contains four twisted pairs of insulated copper conductors — eight conductors total — terminated at each end with an 8-position, 8-contact modular plug colloquially called an **RJ45 connector**. The entire system is governed by two complementary families of standards: **IEEE 802.3**, which defines the Ethernet protocol and physical-layer signaling requirements, and **ANSI/TIA-568**, which defines the cabling infrastructure, connector specifications, and installation practices.[^ieee2022][^tia2020] IEEE tells equipment what to do; TIA tells the cabling what to support.

Ethernet cabling has evolved over fifty years from a 2.94 Mbit/s coaxial bus to a 40 Gbps shielded twisted-pair infrastructure in data center racks, driven by successive generations of IEEE standards and complementary TIA cable category specifications. Throughout that evolution, the fundamental operating principle has remained unchanged: differential signaling over twisted copper pairs, with common-mode noise rejection providing immunity to electromagnetic interference.

## Physical construction

The conductors in a twisted-pair cable are typically 22 to 24 AWG solid bare copper for permanent horizontal runs, or stranded copper for flexible patch cords. 23 AWG has become the dominant gauge for Category 6 and Category 6A. Each conductor is insulated with **high-density polyethylene (HDPE)**, chosen for its low dielectric constant of approximately 2.3, which minimizes capacitance and signal loss at high frequencies. Plenum-rated cables substitute **fluorinated ethylene propylene (FEP)** insulation to meet fire-safety codes for air-handling spaces. The National Electrical Code classifies outer jackets as CM (general use), CMR (riser), or CMP (plenum) based on flame and smoke characteristics.[^tia2020]

The four pairs are color-coded per TIA-568: white/blue and blue, white/orange and orange, white/green and green, white/brown and brown. Each pair is assigned a distinct twist rate — a different number of twists per unit length — which is the primary mechanism for controlling crosstalk between pairs. In Category 6 and higher cables, a plastic cross-web spline or longitudinal separator physically isolates the four pairs to further reduce internal coupling.

Shielding separates twisted-pair cables into several construction types, formally designated in ISO/IEC 11801 notation as X/YTP, where X indicates the overall cable shield and Y indicates the individual pair shield. **U/UTP** (unshielded twisted pair) has no shielding and is the most widely deployed construction in North America for Category 5e, 6, and many 6A installations. **F/UTP** wraps all four pairs in a single aluminum-polyester foil with a drain wire. **U/FTP** places individual foil shields around each pair without an overall screen. **S/FTP** combines a braided copper screen around the entire cable with individual pair foils, providing the highest shielding level and required by Category 7 and Category 8.2.[^iso2017] All shielded cables require proper grounding at both ends; an ungrounded shield can act as an antenna and degrade rather than improve performance.

## How twisting works

The operating principle is **differential signaling with common-mode rejection**. Each pair carries a signal as a voltage difference between its two conductors: one wire carries the positive signal and the other carries an equal but inverted copy. At the receiving end, a differential amplifier subtracts one from the other, doubling the desired signal while canceling any noise induced equally on both wires. Alexander Graham Bell described this principle in his 1881 patent, noting that "if the direct and return wire were affected equally the current generated in one would neutralize and destroy that created in the other."[^bell1881]

Twisting makes this cancellation effective in practice. Because the two conductors in a pair continually swap positions as they spiral, each conductor spends an equal amount of length closer to and farther from any external noise source — a power cable, a fluorescent ballast, an electric motor. The induced noise on each conductor averages to nearly identical values, becoming common-mode noise that the differential receiver rejects. Twisting also minimizes the electromagnetic loop area between the conductors, which reduces both susceptibility to external magnetic fields and the cable's own radiated emissions.

**Crosstalk** — unwanted coupling between pairs within the same cable — is managed by assigning each pair a distinct twist rate. If two adjacent pairs shared the same rate, the same conductors would repeatedly align in the same spatial relationship, creating sustained electromagnetic coupling. Different twist rates continuously randomize the geometric relationship between pairs, preventing persistent alignment. Higher cable categories specify tighter twist rates and more precise manufacturing tolerances to maintain this performance at higher frequencies. Category 6A cables additionally specify **alien crosstalk (ANEXT)**, which is interference between adjacent cables in a bundled pathway — a parameter not controlled by Cat 6, and the principal reason Cat 6 cannot reliably support 10 Gbps beyond about 55 meters.[^tia2020][^fluke2018]

## Cable categories

Each cable category defines transmission performance over a specified frequency range and is associated with specific Ethernet applications and maximum segment lengths.

| Category | Bandwidth | Max speed (copper) | Max channel | Notes |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|-------------|-------|
| Cat 5e | 100 MHz | 1 Gbps | 100 m | Current minimum for Gigabit; being phased out of new commercial work |
| Cat 6 | 250 MHz | 10 Gbps at 55 m / 1 Gbps at 100 m | 100 m | Cross-web spline standard; supports 2.5G/5G at full distance |
| Cat 6A | 500 MHz | 10 Gbps | 100 m | TIA-recommended minimum for new commercial cabling; required for PoE++ |
| Cat 7 | 600 MHz | 10 Gbps | 100 m | ISO/IEC only; no TIA recognition; S/FTP required; GG45/TERA connectors |
| Cat 8 | 2,000 MHz | 25/40 Gbps | 30 m | Data-center racks only; Cat 8.1 uses RJ45; Cat 8.2 uses GG45/TERA |

**Category 5e** was introduced around 1999 and formalized in TIA-568-B.2 in 2001, tightening the power-sum crosstalk and return loss specifications of the original Category 5. It remains adequate for Gigabit Ethernet but cannot support 10 Gbps at any practical distance and its thermal characteristics are marginal for high-wattage Power over Ethernet deployments.[^tia2020]

**Category 6**, ratified in TIA/EIA-568-B.2-1, doubles the bandwidth to 250 MHz. Its 10 Gbps range limitation to 55 meters is caused by alien crosstalk, a parameter not specified in the standard. At Gigabit speeds it supports the full 100-meter channel, and it supports the 2.5GBASE-T and 5GBASE-T multigigabit standards defined in IEEE 802.3bz at full distance.[^ieee2016]

**Category 6A**, ratified in ANSI/TIA-568-B.2-10 in 2008, adds alien crosstalk specifications that Cat 6 omits, enabling reliable 10 Gbps at the full 100-meter channel. It is the current TIA-recommended minimum for all new commercial structured cabling and is essential for IEEE 802.3bt PoE++ (Type 3 and Type 4), which delivers up to 90 W per port and generates significant heat in bundled pathways.[^tia2020][^fluke2018]

**Category 7** is defined exclusively by ISO/IEC 11801 as Class F and is not recognized by TIA. It mandates S/FTP shielding and was designed for use with GG45 or TERA connectors. Most cables marketed as "Cat 7 with RJ45 connectors" are functionally equivalent to Cat 6A under TIA testing methodology and carry no additional benefit.[^iso2017]

**Category 8** operates to 2 GHz and supports 25GBASE-T and 40GBASE-T per IEEE 802.3bq, but only over a 30-meter maximum channel. Cat 8.1 uses standard RJ45 connectors and is backward-compatible with lower categories; Cat 8.2 uses GG45 or TERA connectors. The short range makes Cat 8 purpose-built for data-center top-of-rack connections — server to switch within the same or adjacent racks — and impractical for general horizontal cabling.[^tia2020][^ieee2016]

## The RJ45 connector

The connector universally known as "RJ45" is technically an **8P8C (8 Position, 8 Contact) modular connector**. The "RJ45" name is a misnomer inherited from the Bell System: the original Registered Jack 45 was a keyed 8-position, 2-contact connector for modem circuits. The unkeyed 8P8C plug used for Ethernet is a distinct device that adopted the name through industry convention. Its physical dimensions are specified by ANSI/TIA-1096-A and ISO 8877, and its high-frequency electrical performance requirements are governed by the IEC 60603-7 series.[^iec2020]

The plug contains eight flat metal contacts arranged in a row at 1.02 mm pitch. A plastic latching tab secures the connection to the jack. Pin numbering runs 1 through 8 from left to right when the plug is held with contacts facing up and the latch facing down. For female jacks mounted on wall plates or equipment, the numbering is mirrored when viewed face-on so that pin 1 on the plug always mates with pin 1 on the jack.

Termination is performed by **crimping**, an insulation-displacement process. The installer strips the outer jacket, untwists and orders the eight conductors by color code, trims them evenly, and slides them into the plug body. A crimping tool then forces each of the eight metal contacts through the wire insulation and into the copper conductor, simultaneously compressing the plug body against the jacket for strain relief. Industry practice limits untwisting to no more than 12.7 mm (0.5 in) at the termination point to preserve crosstalk performance.[^tia2020] Plugs are manufactured separately for solid and stranded conductors with different contact geometries; mismatching results in unreliable connections.

## T568A and T568B wiring standards

The two wiring schemes defined by ANSI/TIA-568 — **T568A** and **T568B** — specify the order in which the eight color-coded conductors are assigned to the eight pins of the 8P8C plug. The only difference between them is the swap of the orange pair (Pair 2) and the green pair (Pair 3).

In T568A, pin assignments from 1 through 8 are: white/green, green, white/orange, blue, white/blue, orange, white/brown, brown. In T568B: white/orange, orange, white/green, blue, white/blue, green, white/brown, brown. The blue pair (pins 4 and 5) and brown pair (pins 7 and 8) are identical in both schemes.[^tia2020]

T568A descends from the Universal Service Order Code (USOC) wiring used by the Bell System for telephone service, giving it backward compatibility with one- and two-pair telephone wiring. T568B descends from the **AT&T 258A** (Systimax) standard, which was the most widely installed wiring pattern in commercial premises when TIA-568 was first drafted in 1991; it was included to accommodate existing AT&T installations and through inertia became the dominant scheme in U.S. commercial environments.[^tia2020][^fluke2018] Both standards produce electrically identical performance. ANSI/TIA-568.2-D (2018) is neutral on the choice and requires only that one standard be applied consistently throughout an installation.

## Straight-through and crossover cables

A **straight-through cable** uses the same wiring standard on both ends, creating a one-to-one pin mapping from end to end. It is the standard cable for connecting dissimilar device types: a host (MDI port) to a switch (MDI-X port). In 10BASE-T and 100BASE-TX, an MDI device transmits on pins 1 and 2 and receives on pins 3 and 6, while an MDI-X device does the reverse; the straight-through cable aligns each transmitter with the corresponding receiver.

A **crossover cable** uses T568A on one end and T568B on the other, swapping the orange and green pairs so that the transmit pins on one end connect to the receive pins on the other. It was historically necessary to connect like devices directly — computer to computer, switch to switch — without an intervening switch or hub.

The crossover cable became largely obsolete with **Auto-MDI/MDIX**, a feature that allows a port to detect the connected device's configuration and swap its transmit and receive pairs internally. Auto-MDI/MDIX became mandatory in the IEEE 802.3ab Gigabit Ethernet specification, meaning every compliant 1000BASE-T port supports it.[^ieee1999] Virtually all modern switches, routers, and network interface cards implement it by default, and either cable type functions correctly with any combination of devices as long as autonegotiation is enabled.

## Historical development

Ethernet originated on May 22, 1973, when Robert Metcalfe circulated a memo at Xerox PARC proposing a broadcast packet-switching network inspired by the University of Hawaii's ALOHAnet. The first experimental system ran at 2.94 Mbit/s over thick coaxial cable. Metcalfe and David Boggs published the foundational paper in *Communications of the ACM* in 1976.[^metcalfe1976] In 1980, the DIX consortium (Digital Equipment, Intel, and Xerox) published the first 10 Mbit/s specification, and IEEE formalized it as 802.3 in June 1983 — first as 10BASE5 Thicknet, then as 10BASE2 Thinnet in 1985.[^ieee1985]

The transition to twisted pair arrived with **10BASE-T** (IEEE 802.3i, 1990), which ran at 10 Mbit/s over unshielded twisted pair in a star topology with 100-meter maximum segments. 10BASE-T leveraged existing telephone cabling infrastructure and introduced the 8P8C plug that has defined Ethernet ever since. TIA published the first edition of TIA-568 in July 1991, codifying the structured cabling architecture — horizontal runs, backbone links, telecommunications closets — that gave 10BASE-T a standardized installation framework.[^tia1991]

**100BASE-TX** (IEEE 802.3u, 1995) brought Fast Ethernet over two pairs of Category 5 cable, introducing autonegotiation. **1000BASE-T** (IEEE 802.3ab, 1999) used all four pairs bidirectionally with 4D-PAM5 encoding and required what would become Category 5e.[^ieee1999] **10GBASE-T** (IEEE 802.3an, 2006) drove the development of Category 6A to support its full 100-meter channel.[^ieee2006] **25GBASE-T and 40GBASE-T** (IEEE 802.3bq, 2016) prompted the Category 8 specification, capping the current copper twisted-pair roadmap at 40 Gbps over 30 meters.[^ieee2016]

Parallel to rising speeds, **Power over Ethernet** has expanded the cable's role: IEEE 802.3af (2003) delivered 15.4 W, 802.3at (2009) raised it to 30 W, and **802.3bt** (2018) pushed to 90 W using all four pairs — powering Wi-Fi access points, digital signage, and LED lighting over the same cable that carries their data.[^ieee2018] This convergence of power and data delivery established Category 6A as the baseline for new installations, as its thermal and alien crosstalk characteristics are designed to handle bundled high-wattage PoE deployments safely.
